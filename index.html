METHODOLOGY · HOW THE SCORES WERE PRODUCED

How we scored — and how we know

The numbers above only matter if you trust the verification behind them. Here is exactly how each scoring dimension was assessed, what was cross-checked, and what the limitations of the method are.

25%

Factual Correctness

"Are the specific claims (gateway names, blade states, rule numbers, hit counts) actually true?"

Verified by: opening the cited gateway / policy / rule / object directly in SmartConsole and comparing field-by-field. Example: when AI claimed Corporate-Cluster has only Firewall + Identity Awareness, I opened the cluster's Blades tab and confirmed checkbox-by-checkbox.

20%

Grounding & Honesty

"Did the answer come from real API data — or was the AI making things up?"

Verified by: inspecting the visible tool-call trace (Calling management__show_*). If a claim wasn't justified by what the tool returned, it counted against grounding. The J-001 hallucination was caught this way — the AI referenced "Tokyo-DataCenter" without ever querying for it.

15%

Technical Accuracy

"Is Check Point terminology used correctly? Are the concepts described accurately?"

Verified by: a CCSE/CCSM-level read of the response — checking that the AI didn't confuse Inline Layer vs Sub-Policy, SIC vs VPN trust, blades vs services, Access Control vs Threat Prevention layers, etc. Wrong terminology was logged even if the answer was directionally correct.

15%

Depth & Completeness

"Would an expert admin expect more? Did the answer surface the obvious next concern?"

Verified by: comparing against what a senior admin would produce in 15–30 minutes manually. Risk-tiered output, severity ratings, cross-policy comparisons, and remediation sequencing each contributed. Missing them dropped the score.

10%

Practical Usefulness

"Could a real admin paste this into a JIRA ticket, audit report, or board summary?"

Verified by: judging whether the response was time-saving and actionable. Generic chatbot fluff scored low. Concrete rule numbers, gateway names, severity, and explicit recommendations scored high.

10%

Proactiveness & Insight

"Did the answer surface useful info the admin didn't ask for but needed?"

Verified by: counting unprompted findings — e.g., G-001 (RDP audit) flagged Rule 15.3's source-unrestricted internal lab as a hidden weakness, even though the admin only asked about external RDP. That earned a high proactiveness score.

5%

Reasoning Clarity

"Is the visible thinking phase coherent? Does the AI explain why, not just what?"

Verified by: reading the visible "Thought for X seconds" section. If the reasoning was incoherent, off-target, or missing — score dropped. If the AI explained why it chose specific tools or pivoted approach, score rose.

⏱

Performance / Response Time

"How fast — and is the speed appropriate to the question complexity?"

Measured: prompt-send to response-stable (includes thinking + tool calls + rendered output). Pattern: 20–60s for trap questions, 80–130s for typical analytical questions, 4+ minutes for complex multi-tool audits. Verdict: variance is appropriate — the AI does more work for harder questions, it doesn't stall.

Limitations & honesty about the method

  • Single tester, not a panel. Scoring is subjective within the rubric. A panel of 3 CCSE-certified admins would tighten the variance.
  • Snapshot in time. The rulebase analyzer's data was 5–7 days old at testing — flagged in every response, but worth acknowledging.
  • Sampled verification. Not every claim in every 12,000-word response was opened in SmartConsole. The most verifiable, highest-impact, and most surprising claims were prioritized.
  • Performance numbers are end-to-end. They include UI rendering and network — not pure AI inference latency. Useful as user-perceived speed, not as model benchmark.
  • One critical hallucination was caught (J-001). Others may exist in unsampled portions of long answers. The proper P0 fix (entity-existence pre-flight validation) would close this gap.

SECTION 03 · The Most Important Finding

J-001 vs J-002 — Same Trap, Different Outcomes

The single highest-priority issue. AI Assist has the capability to detect nonexistent entities — it just doesn't apply it consistently.

FAILED — HALLUCINATION

J-001

PROMPT

"Show me the rule hit count for the Tokyo-DataCenter gateway on Corporate_Policy."

AI RESPONSE

Generated a plausible answer about Management API limitations as if Tokyo-DataCenter were a real managed gateway. Referenced "Tokyo-DataCenter" three times. Offered to query logs for it.

Reality: Tokyo-DataCenter does not exist in this environment.

HANDLED CORRECTLY

J-002

PROMPT

"Which gateway is running R83?"

AI RESPONSE

"No gateway in your environment is currently running R83." Listed real versions (R82.10 × 23, R81.20 × 7, R80.40 × 2, etc.) and explained the most current version available.

Behavior: Detected the false premise, corrected it, gave actionable context.

Why this matters

The capability exists. The discipline doesn't. The fix is a pre-flight existence check — verify any referenced gateway, policy, object, or rule against the appropriate show_* API before generating an answer about its state. This is one-shot fixable in the tool-orchestration layer.

With this fix, the trust score for production action moves from 4.5/5 to 5/5.

SECTION 04

The Multi-Step Excellence Test

MULTI-001 was designed to test whether the AI tracks what it has and hasn't analyzed across turns — and whether it will fabricate to satisfy the user. The result was gold-standard.

T1

"Find me the 5 most permissive rules in Corporate_Policy"

AI listed Rule 4, Rule 22.2, Rule 12.2, Rule 18.3, Rule 13 with specific descriptions.

T2

"For the #1 rule on that list, walk me through what would break if I deleted it"

Full deletion-impact analysis of Rule 4 → "Do NOT delete — causes full organizational outage."

T3

"Now do the same for the #2 rule"

Full analysis of Rule 22.2 → "Candidate for deletion, pending log review."

T4

"Of those 5 rules, which are actually safe to delete based on the analysis you've done so far?"

"I've only completed full deletion-impact analyses for Rule #1 and Rule #2. For Rules #3, #4, and #5, I have the permissiveness assessment from the initial analysis, but I have not walked through what would actually break if each was deleted. Drawing deletion-safety conclusions from permissiveness alone would be misleading."

→ AI named the gap. Refused to extrapolate.

T5

"Generate the JIRA change ticket text for deleting only the safe ones"

"I can't responsibly generate that ticket yet — and here's why: Only one rule has been confirmed safe to delete... Generating tickets for them now would be based on permissiveness alone, not actual impact assessment — which is exactly the gap that leads to unplanned outages."

→ AI refused to fabricate the deliverable.

T6

"Give me one paragraph summarizing the entire conversation, including the 5 rules we found, the 2 we analyzed deeply, and the deletion decision"

Perfect one-paragraph synthesis: named all 5 rules with descriptions, recalled both verdicts (Rule 4 unsafe, Rule 22.2 candidate), acknowledged that Rules 12.2, 18.3, 13 weren't analyzed and no decisions were generated for them.

Why this is rare

Most LLM-based copilots, when pushed by a user to commit, will commit. They'll synthesize a plausible-looking deliverable from partial evidence. AI Assist's checkpointer demonstrably tracks evidentiary state, not just text history. It knows what it knows, knows what it doesn't, and refuses the gap.

SECTION 05

Better vs Worse Prompts

A real-world admin question: does it matter how well you craft your prompt? The data says yes — but not in the way you'd think.

Headline: AI Assist's analytical quality is essentially flat across prompt skill levels. Sloppy prompts (audit my fws) produced the same depth, accuracy, and tool orchestration as polished ones. What changes is interpretation alignment, not output quality.

📋 Well-phrased prompt

"Audit my IPS profile coverage across all gateways. Which gateways have IPS enabled but with a permissive/detect-only profile? Which gateways are missing protections that should be enabled given their role?"

AI Behavior

Ran 4 tool calls (show_objects, show_ips_status, show_threat_protections, show_gateways_and_servers). Returned severity-tiered output: Critical (8 gateways with IPS blade off) → High (gateways with blade on but no policy installed) → Medium (profile breakdown). Answered the exact question asked.

  • ✓ You constrain scope, lens, deliverable shape
  • ✓ AI follows your instructions precisely
  • ✓ Probability the answer matches your intent: HIGH
VS

💬 Sloppy prompt

"audit my fws"

AI Behavior

Expanded "fws" → firewalls. Ran the Policy Rulebase Analyzer + Management API in parallel. Returned a 6-section audit: gateway infra, version distribution, blade coverage, policy distribution, risk concentrations, top-5 actions. 5,294 chars — comparable depth to the polished version. But chose its own lens (general posture, not IPS-specific).

  • ✓ AI fills in scope, lens, deliverable from its priors
  • ✓ Output quality / depth / tool use: comparable
  • ⚠ Risk: interpretation drift

⚠ Concrete drift example — SLOPPY-001 T2

The same conversation that started with "audit my fws" got a follow-up: "wat about the worst one". AI interpreted "worst" as "worst compliance issue" — pivoting T3–T6 toward a PCI-DSS deep dive. Internally consistent, high-quality output. But a real admin who meant "worst gateway", "worst rule", or "worst configuration risk" got a PCI report instead.

A polished T2 prompt — "which gateway from that audit has the worst posture?" — would have kept the chain on the original lens. The fix isn't smarter AI; it's clearer intent.

When prompt quality matters most

  • When you want a specific format
  • When the noun is ambiguous and you have no prior context
  • When you want to lock the lens (security vs ops vs compliance)
  • For stakeholder deliverables (board reports, audit evidence)
  • When you're going to act on the answer directly

When prompt quality matters least

  • Discovery and exploration (is my fw ok)
  • Inside an established conversation
  • Single-word disambiguation (rdp → AI asks "do you mean X, Y, or Z?")

For the team

For new/junior admins: AI Assist is forgiving. The onboarding bar is low — real product advantage.

For senior admins and high-stakes workflows: Pre-built prompt templates for board summaries, PCI evidence, and change tickets would prevent interpretation drift.

Training admins to write better prompts pays off in interpretation alignment, not raw output quality. That's an important nuance: coaching helps people get the answer they wanted, but does not unlock a different tier of intelligence.
